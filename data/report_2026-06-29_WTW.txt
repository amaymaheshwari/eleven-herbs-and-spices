## SECTION 10: PRICING POWER AND ELASTICITY TESTING

**[Fact]** Insurance brokerage commissions are typically a percentage of premium placed (standard commercial lines: 5–20% commission rate depending on line and market). In hard market cycles (2019–2023), rising insurance premiums automatically increased brokerage revenue without rate increases — a form of embedded pricing power.

**[Analysis]** WTW has demonstrated pricing power through:
1. **Pass-through commission inflation:** Hard P&C market 2019–2023 drove ~5–8% annual revenue lift in CRB without volume growth
2. **Advisory fee increases:** Annual contract renewals allow 3–5% fee escalations on retainer contracts
3. **Survey subscription pricing:** Annual survey price increases of ~3–5% with limited churn (high switching cost)

**Elasticity Risk:**
- Soft P&C market (2024 onwards in some lines): Flat/declining premium rates reduce commission revenue mechanically
- Benefits consulting: Client price sensitivity is high in economic downturns; discretionary advisory can be deferred
- Benefits administration: Long-term contracts limit near-term repricing but also cap upside

**[Inference]** ARPU ceiling before churn inflects: For large corporate CRB clients, relationship value and switching cost support 5–8% annual pricing above CPI. Above ~10% above CPI, RFP activity accelerates meaningfully. No published elasticity study available for WTW specifically.

**Monetization Change Log:**
- 2021: Divested Willis Re to Gallagher for $3.25B; exit from reinsurance brokerage reduces revenue diversity
- 2022–2023: Transformation program includes product rationalization, reducing lower-margin service lines
- 2024: Continued shift toward higher-margin advisory and data analytics revenue

**Funding Shock Risk:** A large M&A transaction (>$3–5B) could push leverage to 3.0–3.5× and risk a ratings downgrade to BBB-/Baa3. This is the primary capital structure risk.

**Liquidity Runway:** WTW maintains a revolving credit facility of ~$2.5B (largely undrawn). Combined with FCF of ~$1.2–1.3B annually, liquidity is strong. No near-term debt maturity cliff visible.

## SECTION 14: MOAT AND DATA ADVANTAGE

**[Fact]** WTW's competitive moats stem from:

1. **Client relationship depth and switching costs (STRONG):** Multi-year embedded service relationships, RMIS systems, and actuarial data integration create 6–18 month switching barriers for large accounts
2. **Proprietary data assets (MODERATE-STRONG):** Compensation and benefits survey databases (25,000+ organizations), actuarial experience data, claims benchmarking databases
3. **Talent and expertise (MODERATE):** Specialized actuarial, risk management, and benefits advisory expertise; Fellowship of the Actuaries-credentialed professionals are scarce and represent human capital moat
4. **Network effects (WEAK):** WTW does not benefit from strong network effects; the value of its service does not increase directly with user count
5. **Scale economics (MODERATE):** Global placement platform enables better insurance market access and pricing versus smaller brokers

**[Analysis]** The moat is primarily *switching cost* and *talent/expertise* based, not network-effect or data-network compound moat. This is a structurally weaker moat than a technology platform but more durable than pure commodity services.

**Event Most Likely to Collapse the Moat (within 2 years):**
- A major talent exodus in the actuarial/pension division, triggered by compensation gaps or cultural disruption from Transformation layoffs
- InsurTech disintermediation of mid-market commercial lines (less likely at large-account level, but real risk in mid-market by 2026–2028)
