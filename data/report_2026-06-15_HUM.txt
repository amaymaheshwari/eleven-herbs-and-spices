## SECTION 7: GO-TO-MARKET AND DISTRIBUTION

**[Fact]** Medicare Advantage plans are distributed through:
1. Direct-to-consumer (DTC) marketing (~30% of enrollments)
2. Independent insurance agents / brokers (~45%)
3. Captive agents (~15%)
4. Online platforms (eHealth, GoHealth, SelectQuote) (~10%)

**[Fact]** Third-party marketing organizations (TMOs) and lead aggregators have come under CMS regulatory scrutiny in 2024–2025, with new rules limiting agent switching incentives (CMS Final Rule on MA Marketing, April 2024). This structural shift benefits larger direct-distribution insurers.

**[Analysis]** Humana's distribution is heavily broker-dependent. Rising agent commissions and TMO costs have been a documented headwind (Humana Q3 2024 earnings call, November 2024). The CMS marketing rule changes (effective 2025) reduce broker-driven "churn and burn" — potentially stabilizing Humana's membership at the cost of slower growth.

**Biggest funnel bottleneck:** Humana's benefit competitiveness. If $0-premium plans with rich supplemental benefits drive enrollment decisions, Humana's repriced (less generous) 2025–2026 plans face headwinds in broker recommendations. Doubling pipeline without doubling opex requires closing the star-rating gap (enables QBP-funded competitive benefits) — a multi-year, not near-term, solution.

## SECTION 10: PRICING POWER AND ELASTICITY TESTING

**[Analysis]** Humana's "pricing power" in MA is deeply constrained by regulation:

1. **Premium pricing:** Plans can charge $0 premiums (and most do to compete). Effective pricing competition occurs through benefit richness, not premium levels.
2. **Cost-sharing design:** Humana has more flexibility in deductibles, copays, and out-of-pocket maximums — this is where effective repricing occurs in 2025–2026.
3. **CMS benchmark rates:** The true price lever is CMS's annual payment rate, which Humana cannot control.

**[Fact]** For 2025, CMS finalized a net payment rate change of +0.16% for MA plans, while medical cost trend was running 5–8% (CMS Final Rule, April 4, 2024). This rate-cost gap is the root cause of HUM's MLR crisis.

**[Fact]** For 2026, CMS proposed a net rate increase of approximately +2.2% (CMS Advance Notice, February 2025), still below medical cost trend estimates of 4–6%.

**[Inference]** Humana has limited pricing power in the traditional sense. Its "pricing" actions are defensive cost-side adjustments (benefit cuts, network restrictions, higher cost-sharing) that risk member attrition. True pricing power recovery requires CMS rate adequacy or a sustained reduction in medical cost trend — neither is within management's control.

**Reference price and switching cost:** A typical MA member implicitly values plan switching cost at $200–$400 in annual out-of-pocket equivalent (based on research by MedPAC and KFF on plan-switching behavior). This is a low switching cost, limiting premium pricing power.

- This bridges from ~$0–2 current EPS to ~$18–20 normalized EPS — the consensus bull case

**[Fact]** Management guided for full-year 2025 adjusted EPS of approximately $6.00–7.00 (Humana Q4 2024 earnings call, February 2025) — a massive reduction from prior $16+ targets.

**[Inference]** The MLR recovery from 90.4% to 87% requires: (1) benefit redesign taking full effect in plan year 2025–2026, (2) medical cost trend moderation, (3) no adverse selection from member mix deterioration, and (4) star-rating-linked QBP recovery.
