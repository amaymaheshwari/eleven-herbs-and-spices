## SECTION 1: THESIS FRAMING

**[Fact]** Eagle Materials Inc. (NYSE: EXP) is a pure-play U.S. building-materials producer with two reportable segments: Heavy Materials (Portland cement and concrete/aggregates) and Light Materials (wallboard/gypsum and paperboard). For fiscal year ending March 31, 2025 (FY2025), EXP generated approximately $2.05B in net revenues, ~$650M in EBITDA (estimated ~32% margin), and ~$400M in free cash flow (FCF). The company has repurchased approximately 50% of its shares outstanding over the past decade, reducing the share count from ~60M in FY2015 to approximately 47–48M as of FY2025.

**[Analysis]** The value-creation hurdle: EXP must sustain pricing power across cement and wallboard, deploy FCF into buybacks at accretive prices, and benefit from infrastructure and housing demand recovery—generating a 30%+ total return in 24 months versus the S&P 500.

### 2.3 Supply Constraint Analysis

**[Fact]** U.S. cement industry capacity utilization has run at 80–90%+ in recent years per PCA (Portland Cement Association) data, representing a tight supply environment. New greenfield cement plants require 5–7 years to permit and build, with capital costs of $500M–$1B+ per plant. This structural supply rigidity is the primary enabler of EXP's pricing power.

**[Inference]** Demand, not supply, is the binding constraint in EXP's core cement markets through at least FY2027. In wallboard, supply is less constrained (faster to add capacity), making demand recovery in housing the key variable.

### 3.1 Customer Pain Points and Switching Costs

**[Analysis]** For cement customers, switching costs are material but not prohibitive:
- **Geographic lock-in**: Cement is uneconomical to transport >300 miles by truck; customers within EXP's radius have limited alternatives
- **Quality consistency**: DOT specifications require certified suppliers; EXP's approvals in its regions represent barriers
- **Supply reliability**: Large contractors value supply certainty; relationships and allocation priority matter in tight markets

For wallboard customers:
- **Switching costs are lower**: Wallboard is a commodity; builders can switch between USG, Georgia-Pacific, and EXP based on price/availability
- **Logistics relationships**: EXP's plant locations near key Sun Belt markets provide delivery-cost advantages
- **Pricing sensitivity**: Homebuilders are highly price-sensitive on commodity inputs

**[Inference]** Cement customers have moderate switching costs (geographic + regulatory); wallboard customers have low switching costs. EXP's moat in cement is primarily geographic/logistical; in wallboard it is cost-based scale.

- Wallboard: Price and availability; no meaningful non-price blocker

### 10.1 Pricing Power Analysis

**[Analysis]** Cement pricing power is structurally high because:
1. Geographic oligopoly in each local market
2. No viable substitutes for Portland cement in structural applications
3. High capacity utilization constrains ability to discount for volume
4. Cost-push (energy, labor) provides cover for price increases
5. Historical: cement prices rarely fall more than 5% in any year; major producers coordinate pricing through public price-increase announcements (not collusion, as announcements are public and follow industry practice)

**Wallboard pricing power is cyclically dependent:**
- In housing up-cycles: strong pricing power (all 3–4 major producers operate at capacity)
- In housing down-cycles: significant price concessions; 2022–2024 correction of ~25–30% demonstrates elasticity

**[Inference]** EXP's composite pricing power is moderate-to-strong (blending high-moat cement with cyclical wallboard). ARPU ceiling before churn inflects is not applicable in traditional sense; EXP monitors competitor pricing closely before implementing increases.
