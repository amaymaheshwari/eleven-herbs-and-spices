**[Fact]** U.S. household formation among the 25–44 age cohort (prime renting years) is projected at approximately 1.2–1.4 million annually through 2028 (Harvard Joint Center for Housing Studies, "State of the Nation's Housing 2024," June 2024). The homeownership affordability gap in AVB's core markets remains near historic extremes: in San Jose, CA, the monthly cost of buying a median-priced home (~$1.5M) exceeds the equivalent apartment rent by $3,000–$4,500/month (National Association of Realtors / Zillow, Q1 2025). This structural affordability gap is a durable demand driver.

**[Fact]** Migration patterns: While coastal-to-Sun Belt migration slowed in 2023–2024 vs 2020–2022 peaks (USPS Change-of-Address data, 2024), coastal gateway markets have seen employment recovery and immigration-driven household formation support demand (U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics, Metropolitan Employment Report, Q1 2025).

### Supply Constraint Analysis

**[Fact / Analysis]** The binding constraint in AVB's markets is supply, not demand. New apartment construction faces (1) restrictive zoning, (2) NIMBYism, (3) high land/construction costs, and (4) elevated financing costs. CoStar data (May 2025) shows completions in the top 6 AVB coastal markets running 15–25% below 2023–2024 peaks already. This is the most critical near-term catalyst for SSRG improvement.

**TAM Shrinkage Scenario:** If remote work causes permanent 5–10% reduction in demand for high-cost coastal apartments, AVB's effective SAM contracts by ~300,000–600,000 households. At AVB's ~$2,800/month average rent, this implies a $10–17B annual rental revenue pool loss in its SAM — not existential, but a headwind to rent growth in 2026–2028.

## SECTION 3: CUSTOMER SEGMENTS AND JOBS

### Renter Profile

**[Fact]** AVB's target renter is the "high-quality renter by choice" — primarily Millennial and Gen Z professionals, dual-income-no-kids (DINKs), and empty-nesters choosing rental flexibility over homeownership. Average household income for new AVB lease signers is approximately $135,000–$155,000 (AVB Investor Day 2024; Inference based on typical Class A renter demographics). Average monthly rent was $2,831 as of Q4 2024 (AVB Q4 2024 Earnings Release, February 2025).

**[Analysis]** Rent-to-income ratio: $2,831 × 12 / $145,000 household income = ~23.4%. This is within the conventional 30% affordability threshold, suggesting AVB's renter base has meaningful income coverage headroom. Rent-to-income at the margin may stretch to 28–30% before churn inflects materially.

### Customer Jobs and Pain Points

| Job to Be Done | AVB Solution | Switching Cost | Mission-Criticality |
|---|---|---|---|
| High-quality urban/suburban housing near employment | Modern amenities, responsive management, good locations | Moderate — moving costs $2,000–$5,000 + deposits | High — primary housing need |

**[Analysis]** Switching costs for apartment renters are moderate but real. In AVB's high-cost markets, the friction of moving (cost, time, deposits, broker fees in NYC at 1 month's rent = ~$2,800) provides meaningful retention. AVB's lease renewal rate historically runs ~55–60%, with approximately 40–45% of move-outs due to home purchase (increasingly constrained), job relocation, or downsizing (AVB Supplemental Data, 2024).

### Do-Nothing Prevalence

**[Analysis]** "Do-nothing" (renting elsewhere or staying in place) is the primary competitive dynamic. In weak markets, concessions (1–2 months free rent) become the norm. In Q1 2025, AVB reported concession usage of approximately 4–6% of new leases in its Seattle and some California markets (Inference from SSR commentary on Q1 2025 earnings call, April 2025).

### Procurement Blocker

**[Analysis]** The primary "procurement blocker" for new tenants is affordability. At $2,831/month average, a renter needs ~$95,000 in qualifying income (33× rent rule). In markets where AVB rents exceed $3,500–$4,000/month (San Francisco, NYC), the qualifying income threshold of $115,000–$132,000 limits the addressable renter pool. This is a structural ceiling on pricing power in highest-rent markets.

## SECTION 10: PRICING POWER AND ELASTICITY TESTING

### Pricing Governance

**[Fact]** AVB uses a proprietary dynamic pricing/revenue management system (similar to LRO — Lease Rent Options — but in-house) that adjusts individual unit pricing daily based on occupancy, lease expiration schedules, competitor pricing, and macro inputs. **[Analysis]** Revenue management sophistication is a competitive advantage. Class A operators using algorithmic revenue management consistently show 75–150 bps higher revenue yield vs non-RM operators (National Apartment Association / NMHC research, 2023).

### Elasticity Evidence

**[Fact]** Rent-to-income ratio: At AVB's average rent of $2,831 and typical new renter household income ~$145,000, the current affordability ratio (~23%) is well below the historical inflection point of ~30%, suggesting approximately 25–30% additional pricing headroom before churn accelerates (Analysis: $145,000 × 30% / 12 = $3,625 theoretical ceiling vs current $2,831).

### Development Economics

**[Fact]** AVB's core value creation engine is its development program. Typical development metrics (AVB Investor Day 2024 and Q1 2025 Supplemental):

**Development Value Creation Math:**

**[Analysis]** This is genuinely attractive capital allocation if (a) construction costs stay contained and (b) cap rates compress or hold. At current cap rates (~4.9%) and cost levels, development creates meaningful value. The risk: if cap rates rise 50 bps, stabilized value = $10.44M / 0.054 = $193.3M — only $13.3M profit on $180M cost (7.4% margin) — still positive but much less compelling.
