# SECTION 15: DATA AND AI ECONOMICS

**Analysis.** CBT is not a data or AI business in the conventional sense. However, data advantages exist:

# SECTION 17: SUPPLY CHAIN AND OPERATIONS

**Fact.** Carbon black production requires coal tar or heavy aromatic oil (CBFS) feedstock derived from petroleum refining. CBT is one of the world's largest buyers of this feedstock. Key supply chain risks:

### Critical Supplier Map

| Input | Supplier Type | Concentration Risk | Mitigation |
|---|---|---|---|
| Carbon Black Feedstock (CBFS) | Oil refinery co-products | Medium (3–5 primary suppliers per region) | Multi-supplier contracts; some co-location |
| Cesium ore (for fluids) | Mining (limited global sources) | High (few commercial cesium sources globally) | Long-term contracts; recycling program (cesium formate is recycled from wellbore returns) |
| Natural gas (energy) | Utility/pipeline | Low-medium | Regional diversification |
| Specialty chemicals (activated carbon activation agents) | Multiple | Low | Multiple qualified suppliers |

**Analysis.** The single point of failure in the supply chain is **cesium ore supply** for the Specialty Fluids segment. However, this segment is ~5% of revenue. The company has a cesium formate recycling program (recovering and reprocessing used drilling fluid) that partially insulates it from virgin ore supply constraints.

**Manufacturing economics:** Carbon black production is energy-intensive. Energy represents approximately 15–20% of COGS. Natural gas price increases of +$1/MMBtu increase annual costs by approximately $15–25M. CBT's LTA pass-through mechanisms cover most energy cost movements, but with a lag of 3–6 months.

**Yield learning rate vs. peers:** Not publicly benchmarked. Based on disclosed energy efficiency improvements over 5 years, CBT has reduced energy intensity per MT by approximately 3–5%, in line with industry best practices.
