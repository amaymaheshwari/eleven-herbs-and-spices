# SECTION 1: THESIS FRAMING

**Fact:** Matador Resources (founded 2003, public since 2012) is a Dallas-based independent E&P (Exploration & Production) company focused on the Delaware Basin (a sub-basin of the Permian Basin in West Texas and southeastern New Mexico), with additional legacy operations in Eagle Ford (South Texas) and Haynesville (Louisiana). As of Q1 2025, average daily production was approximately 173,000 barrels of oil equivalent per day (Mboe/d), with oil comprising roughly 51–53% of total volumes. [Source: MTDR Q1 2025 10-Q filing, May 2025]

**Fact:** San Mateo Midstream, LLC is MTDR's midstream joint venture (JV), in which MTDR holds ~51% and Five Point Capital Partners holds ~49%. San Mateo operates natural gas gathering, compression, treating, and water disposal infrastructure in the Delaware Basin, generating fee-based revenues. [Source: MTDR 2024 Annual Report / 10-K, February 2025]

**Analysis:** The thesis rests on five concrete if-then pillars:

**Thesis Pillars (If-Then Structure)**

1. **IF** Delaware Basin well productivity continues on its observed improvement trajectory (initial production rates increasing ~10–15% YoY per well vintage), **THEN** MTDR's capital efficiency (measured as barrels of oil equivalent (boe) produced per dollar of capital expenditure (capex)) will compress per-boe finding and development (F&D) costs toward $10–12/boe, supporting FCF generation above $800M annually at $70 WTI.

2. **IF** San Mateo Phase III capacity expansion (targeted 1.1 billion cubic feet per day (Bcf/d) processing capacity) comes online in Q4 2025, **THEN** third-party revenue (currently ~35–40% of San Mateo throughput) will grow, providing fee-based earnings insulation from oil price volatility and a distinct valuation anchor.

3. **IF** MTDR's leverage declines from ~1.5× Net Debt/EBITDA (Q1 2025 estimate) toward the stated 1.0× target within 18 months, **THEN** the company will accelerate return of capital (buybacks + dividends), which is a known re-rating catalyst in the E&P sector (peer precedent: Pioneer Natural Resources pre-acquisition, Devon Energy 2021–2022).

4. **IF** the Delaware Basin acreage position (MTDR holds ~150,000 net acres as of year-end 2024) continues to yield inventory life of 15+ years at current pace, **THEN** MTDR deserves a NAV (Net Asset Value) multiple premium versus peers with shorter runway.

5. **IF** bolt-on M&A in the Delaware Basin is completed at ≤5.0× EV (Enterprise Value)/EBITDA (a realistic target given the current private-operator seller universe), **THEN** the transaction will be immediately FCF-accretive and expand the inventory runway, potentially adding $5–8/share of incremental NAV.

# SECTION 3: CUSTOMER SEGMENTS AND JOBS

*Note: As an E&P company, MTDR does not have "customers" in a traditional SaaS sense. This section is adapted to cover: (A) commodity offtake counterparties and (B) San Mateo midstream customers (third-party producers).*

**Fact — Commodity Offtake:**
- MTDR sells crude oil under term contracts (typically 30–90 day rolling) to major refiners and crude marketers including Plains All American, Enterprise Products Partners, and other Permian Basin intermediaries.
- Natural gas and Natural Gas Liquids (NGLs) are sold at the wellhead or through San Mateo to downstream processors and traders.
- Oil price realizations: MTDR typically realizes WTI minus $1.00–2.50/bbl differential in the Delaware Basin, which is favorable versus some peers given proximity to Wink-to-Webster and Permian-Gulf Coast pipeline infrastructure.

**Analysis — San Mateo Third-Party Customers:**
- San Mateo derives approximately 35–40% of throughput from third-party E&P producers operating near MTDR's acreage. These are mission-critical services (gas gathering and water disposal are necessary for operators to produce legally and economically).
- Switching costs are high: changing midstream providers requires new pipeline connections and potentially new water disposal wells. Estimated switching cost: $5–15M per producer depending on volume and distance.
- Do-nothing prevalence: Producers on existing San Mateo connections have very low incentive to switch absent a significant price differential, given the capital cost and time required to redirect volumes.
- Procurement blocker: For new third-party wins, MTDR/San Mateo must demonstrate capacity availability, competitive tariff rates (typically $0.20–0.40/Mcf gas gathering; $0.50–1.00/bbl water disposal), and financial stability of the JV.

**Inference:** San Mateo's third-party revenue stream is the most underappreciated element of MTDR's business model, representing effectively a regulated-utility-like cash flow stream embedded within an E&P company. The market does not adequately price this differentiation.
