## SECTION 1 — THESIS FRAMING

**[Fact]** AT&T Inc. is the second-largest US wireless carrier by subscribers (~116 million total wireless connections as of Q1 2025) and the largest fiber broadband provider by new deployment pace, targeting 30 million fiber passings by end-2025, up from approximately 27.5 million reported at Q1 2025 (AT&T Q1 2025 Earnings Release, April 23, 2025).

**[Fact]** AT&T's 2025 financial guidance (as of April 23, 2025) targets: revenues of $30.6–$31.0B for Mobility, $13.5B+ for Consumer Wireline, adjusted EBITDA of $44–$44.5B, capital expenditure (capex) of $22B, and FCF of $16B+ (AT&T Investor Relations, 2025 Guidance Update).

**[Analysis]** The investment thesis rests on a deceptively simple but historically elusive telecom proposition: AT&T has shed its complex conglomerate structure (WarnerMedia spun off April 2022, DirecTV being divested), which should allow management to allocate capital with surgical focus toward (1) converged wireless + fiber connectivity and (2) deleveraging. The value-creation hurdle is whether $16B+ FCF is durable, growing, and whether leverage can reach 2.5× Net Debt/EBITDA by mid-2026 to unlock return-of-capital optionality.

## SECTION 2 — MARKET STRUCTURE AND SIZE

**[Fact]** The US wireless market comprises approximately 350 million wireless connections (CTIA, 2024 Annual Survey). The postpaid phone segment — the value driver — totals approximately 220 million connections. AT&T holds approximately 95 million postpaid phone connections (~43% share), T-Mobile ~97 million (~44%), Verizon ~92 million (~42%) — note overlaps due to definitional differences (CTIA; company filings Q1 2025).

**[Fact]** The US fiber broadband Total Addressable Market (TAM) is approximately 130 million US households. AT&T's fiber network passes approximately 27.5 million locations as of Q1 2025, targeting 30M by end-2025 and potentially 35–40M by 2027–2028. At a 35% penetration rate, a 30M-passing footprint implies ~10.5M fiber subscribers vs. current ~9.1M (AT&T Q1 2025: 261K net adds in the quarter).

### Market Sizing by Segment

| Segment | US TAM | AT&T Serviceable Market | AT&T Current Revenue | AT&T Market Share |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Postpaid wireless | ~$120B rev p.a. | ~$120B | ~$30B mobility | ~25% of total US wireless rev |
| Fiber broadband | ~$50B rev p.a. (growing) | ~$27.5B at 30M passings × $65 ARPU × 12 | ~$7.2B fiber annualized | ~14% of fiber rev market |
| Business wireline | ~$60B enterprise | ~$25B | ~$14B | ~23% |
| FirstNet public safety | ~$10B (specialized) | $10B (near monopoly) | ~$3B+ | ~60%+ |

**[Fact]** The US wireless market is effectively a three-player oligopoly (AT&T, Verizon, T-Mobile) controlling ~98% of postpaid connections. Cable virtual network operator (MVNO) incursion (Comcast Xfinity Mobile, Charter Spectrum Mobile) is the primary structural threat, now at approximately 12–13 million combined wireless lines (Comcast Q1 2025, Charter Q1 2025).

**[Analysis]** Growth drivers: (1) Fiber broadband TAM is supply-constrained — AT&T can grow by building; (2) Wireless revenue growth is driven by ARPU upgrades (5G premium tiers, FirstNet, IoT) rather than subscriber growth; (3) Fixed wireless access (FWA) from T-Mobile and Verizon creates a ceiling on cable MVNO bundling economics.

**[Inference]** TAM-shrinkage scenario: If cable MVNOs reach 25 million lines by 2027 (doubling from current), and they primarily draw from AT&T/Verizon (T-Mobile's network hosts Comcast MVNO, reducing incentive to take AT&T subs), AT&T postpaid wireless churn could rise by 10–15 bps, reducing annual net adds by ~500K. This would reduce Mobility revenue by ~$300M vs. base case — manageable but worth monitoring.

**Demand vs. Supply Constraint:** Wireless: demand-constrained (market near saturation at ~95% penetration of adults). Fiber: supply-constrained (AT&T can take share wherever it builds and passes homes; overbuild risk is the key variable).

**[Analysis]** Switching costs are highest in:

**[Inference]** The "do-nothing" prevalence is highest among enterprise wireline customers using legacy MPLS networks — a double-edged sword. These generate predictable cash flows but are structurally declining as enterprise shifts to SD-WAN and cloud-native networking. AT&T's managed cloud/networking revenue must offset this secular decline.

**Procurement Blocker in B2B:** IT security approval and total-cost-of-ownership (TCO) modeling. Proof required: AT&T must demonstrate fiber/SD-WAN reduces network cost by ≥15% vs. legacy MPLS to unlock contract renewal at higher ARPA.

**[Inference]** AT&T's ecosystem is a **network-effect-light** model. Switching cost is structural (spectrum, infrastructure) rather than driven by data network effects. This is both a strength (harder to displace) and a weakness (less scalable economically than a software platform).

## SECTION 15 — DATA AND AI ECONOMICS
