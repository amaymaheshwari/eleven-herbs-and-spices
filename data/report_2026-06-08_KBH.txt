## SECTION 10: PRICING POWER AND ELASTICITY TESTING

### Pricing Governance

**Fact.** KB Home sets base prices by community, adjusted monthly based on competitive dynamics, absorption rate targets, and cost inputs. Incentive programs (mortgage rate buydowns, design studio credits, closing cost assistance) are deployed as a pricing lever rather than base-price cuts—preserving ASP optics while compressing net margin.

### Elasticity Evidence

**Analysis.** KB Home's ASP rose from ~$390K (FY2021) to ~$490K (FY2024), a 26% increase over three years, while orders fell in 2023 before recovering. This suggests moderate pricing power: buyers accept higher prices when mortgage rates are low (2021) but become highly elastic when combined mortgage + price increases payment-to-income ratio beyond qualification thresholds.

**Quantified Elasticity:**
- A 1% increase in 30-year mortgage rate ≈ 11% decrease in monthly payment affordability at fixed purchase price
- When monthly payment exceeds ~28–30% of median household income threshold, qualified buyer pool shrinks ~15–20%

### Reference Price and Switching Cost

**Analysis.** Reference price: buyers benchmark against existing-home inventory (~$420K national median existing-home price as of April 2025, per NAR). KB Home new-home premium vs. existing: ~17% ($490K vs. $420K). This premium must be justified by new-home advantages (warranty, energy efficiency, customization). As existing-home inventory remains historically low (lock-in effect from 3% mortgages), the new-home premium has been sustained.

### ARPU Ceiling Before Churn Inflects

**Inference.** At ~7.0% mortgage rate, the maximum sustainable ASP for KB Home's target buyer (median household income ~$85K, assuming 43% total-debt-to-income limit, 20% down) is approximately:
- Max monthly PITI: 0.43 × $85K ÷ 12 = $3,046
- Property taxes + insurance ≈ $800/month
- Max principal + interest: $2,246
- At 7.0%, 30-year: max loan = ~$338K; with 20% down: max home price = ~$422K

**Analysis.** At current rates (~7.0%), KB Home's ~$490K ASP is ABOVE the maximum qualification threshold for a median-income buyer with 20% down—which explains elevated cancellations and incentive spending. This is the core bear case. At 6.0% rates, the max home price rises to ~$465K (10% down scenario), still tight but meaningfully improved. Implication: **every 50-bps rate reduction expands the qualified buyer pool by ~8–10% in KB Home's target segment.**

1. **Capitalized interest:** KB Home capitalizes interest to land inventory; this reduces reported SG&A/interest expense but flows through Cost of Goods Sold (COGS) at delivery. With ~$880M in debt, approximately $50–60M is capitalized annually. A rate-spike scenario inflating capitalized interest would eventually compress gross margin.
2. **Inventory impairments:** If land values decline, impairment charges can swing EBIT by $50–200M in a stress scenario. FY2023 had minimal impairments (~$15M); FY2022 had ~$5M.
3. **Warranty reserve:** ~$45–55M annually; actuarial assumption changes can swing EBIT by ~$10–20M.

## SECTION 17: SUPPLY CHAIN AND OPERATIONS

### Critical Suppliers

**Fact.** Homebuilders rely on:
1. **Lumber:** Southern Yellow Pine and Douglas Fir; spot price highly volatile. Lumber futures (CME Random Length Lumber) peaked ~$1,700/MBF (May 2021), fell to ~$350/MBF (Q4 2023), and are currently ~$500/MBF (June 2025). Each $100/MBF change in lumber price ≈ $1,500–2,000/home cost impact.
2. **OSB/Panel Products:** Critical for framing; concentrated supply (Weyerhaeuser, West Fraser, LP Building Products).
3. **Windows/Doors:** Typically 2–3 primary suppliers per region; lead times normalized to 4–6 weeks (from 16–20 weeks in 2022).
4. **HVAC Systems:** Carrier, Lennox, Trane; adequate supply currently.
5. **Concrete/Masonry:** Local/regional; limited disruption risk currently.

### Single-Source Exposure

**Analysis.** Windows and doors represent the highest single-source exposure risk in KB Home's supply chain—if PGT Innovations (Florida) or JELD-WEN experiences a major operational disruption, cycle times could extend 2–3 months in affected markets.

### Manufacturing Economics / Yield Learning Rate

**Analysis.** KB Home does not manufacture homes; it manages the design + subcontractor build process. "Yield improvement" equivalent is cycle-time reduction: each community that achieves sub-5-month cycle time improves asset turnover and community-level ROI by ~15–20%.

### Cost-Curve vs. Peers

**Analysis.** KB Home's construction cost per square foot (~$135–145/sqft) is above D.R. Horton's (~$115–125/sqft) due to higher customization complexity (BTO). This is a structural cost premium that is partially offset by the higher ASP and upgrade revenue from Design Studio.
