## SECTION 2 — MARKET STRUCTURE AND SIZE

**[Fact]** Global primary aluminum production was approximately 69–70 million metric tons (Mt) in 2024, according to the International Aluminium Institute (IAI). China produced approximately 41–42 Mt (~60% of global output). Ex-China production was approximately 28–29 Mt.

**[Fact]** Global aluminum demand was similarly ~69–70 Mt in 2024, with growth driven by: (1) electric vehicles (EVs) — aluminum content per BEV is approximately 30–35% higher than an internal combustion engine (ICE) vehicle; (2) energy transition infrastructure (solar, wind, grid); (3) construction (largest segment, ~28% of demand); and (4) packaging (~24%).

### Market Size by Segment

| Segment | 2024E Global Demand | Growth Driver | Alcoa Exposure |
|---|---|---|---|
| Transportation (auto, aerospace) | ~27 Mt (~39%) | EV lightweighting; aerospace recovery | Smelting |
| Building & Construction | ~20 Mt (~28%) | Infrastructure stimulus; green building | Smelting |
| Packaging | ~17 Mt (~24%) | Can sheet; sustainability mandates | Warrick divestiture reduced |
| Electrical | ~4 Mt (~6%) | Grid buildout; EV charging | Smelting |
| Other | ~2 Mt (~3%) | Industrial | Smelting |

**[Fact]** Alcoa's 2024 aluminum production was approximately 2.2–2.3 Mt (smelting) and alumina production approximately 9.5–10.0 Mt (refining), giving a global smelting market share of approximately 3.2–3.3% and alumina refining share of approximately 11–12%.

**[Analysis]** The binding constraint is **supply** on a structural basis (capacity cannot quickly respond to price) and **demand** in the short cycle (automotive/construction sensitivity to interest rates and GDP). China's overcapacity is the perennial structural headwind. Ex-China, the industry has been broadly disciplined, with few greenfield smelters economically viable at LME below $2,500/t given power and capex costs.

### TAM Shrinkage Scenarios

- **Aluminum substitution by advanced composites** in aerospace: Minimal risk near-term (<5% of volumes); composites cost 5–10× aluminum.
- **Steel re-entry in auto body panels** if aluminum premiums rise: Moderate risk; but CAFE standards and EV lightweighting mandate aluminum.
- **Recycled/secondary aluminum growth** (scrap): Growing; secondary already ~35% of ex-China supply and growing 2–3% per year. Does NOT directly substitute primary aluminum for aerospace/auto applications requiring high purity. Long-term risk to primary demand is real but manageable through 2027.

## SECTION 4 — PRODUCT AND ROADMAP

**[Fact]** Alcoa's three business segments are: (1) Bauxite, (2) Alumina, and (3) Aluminum. These are physical commodity segments, not software products. The "roadmap" is therefore a portfolio transformation and technology roadmap.

### Core Product Lines

| Segment | 2024 Production Volume | Revenue Contribution | Key Asset |
|---|---|---|---|
| Bauxite | ~48–50 Mt | ~8% of segment revenue (mostly captive) | Huntly/Willowdale (Australia); Juruti (Brazil) |
| Alumina | ~9.5–10.0 Mt | ~40% | Pinjarra, Wagerup, Kwinana (Australia — Kwinana closing); Juruti-linked; AWAC JV |
| Aluminum | ~2.2–2.3 Mt | ~52% | Warrick IN (rolling divested; smelter retained), Iceland (ISAL), Lista (Norway), Portland (Australia), Ferndale WA, Alcoa TN, Newburgh IN, Massena NY |

### Technology Roadmap — ELYSIS

**[Fact]** ELYSIS is a joint venture between Alcoa (50%) and Rio Tinto (50%), with C$188 million in combined government and corporate funding (Government of Canada C$60M, Government of Quebec C$60M, Apple and others). ELYSIS technology eliminates carbon anodes in smelting, producing oxygen instead of CO₂. Alcoa and Rio Tinto announced in May 2024 that a commercial-scale prototype had produced aluminum at the Alma smelter in Quebec. The commercial-scale demonstration cell is targeting ~2026.

**[Fact]** Alcoa's Inert Anode technology (ELYSIS) targets a carbon-free smelting process that would reduce CO₂ emissions by approximately 6.5 tonnes of CO₂ equivalent per tonne of aluminum produced (vs. the current industry average of ~12 kg CO₂ per kg aluminum for primary production). [Source: ELYSIS joint venture press releases, 2024.]

**[Analysis]** ELYSIS is a genuine potential moat if commercialized. However, significant uncertainty remains on: (a) capital cost per tonne of ELYSIS vs. conventional smelting, (b) energy intensity (potentially higher kWh/t), (c) anode material supply chain, (d) retrofit vs. greenfield economics. Timeline risk is high; commercial-scale demonstration ≠ commercial deployment.

### Kwinana Closure

**[Fact]** Alcoa announced the permanent closure of the Kwinana alumina refinery in Western Australia in February 2023, with closure targeting completion in H2 2025. Kwinana had a nameplate capacity of approximately 2.1 Mt/year of alumina but was operating at sub-capacity due to high costs. The closure is expected to reduce Alcoa's Australian alumina production but improve system-wide cost structure by approximately $70–100M in annualized cash savings.

**[Analysis]** Alcoa's cost position is **2nd–3rd quartile** globally, meaning it is not the lowest-cost producer. This is a structural weakness. Alcoa's Australian smelter (Portland) is among the highest-cost in the global fleet and has periodically received government subsidies to remain operational.

**[Fact]** The global aluminum industry's cost curve (per Wood Mackenzie/CRU estimates, 2024) shows the 90th-percentile cash cost at approximately $2,400–2,600/t, meaning at current LME levels (~$2,200–2,350/t), the highest-cost producers are below breakeven. Alcoa's system-wide average cash cost of production was approximately $2,150–2,250/t in 2024 (blended bauxite-alumina-aluminum, net of alumina sales), implying thin margins at current prices.

### Competitive Moat Assessment

**[Inference]** Alcoa's competitive moat is **narrow and primarily positional** (long-lived bauxite reserves in Guinea/Australia, AWAC joint venture structure with Alumina Limited being bought out in 2024). The company does not have the structural power-cost advantage of Rio Tinto or Hydro, making it more cyclically exposed.

**[Fact]** Alcoa completed the acquisition of Alumina Limited (the minority partner in the AWAC joint venture) for approximately AUD 2.2 billion (approximately US$1.4 billion) in August 2024, making AWAC wholly owned. This simplifies corporate structure but added debt.

## SECTION 10 — PRICING POWER AND ELASTICITY TESTING

**[Analysis]** Alcoa is a price-taker on commodity aluminum and a partial price-influencer on alumina (given ~12% global market share). Pricing power is **structurally low** in standard aluminum; **moderate** in specialty alumina grades; and **potentially high** for certified green/ELYSIS aluminum (in future).

**[Fact]** The alumina market saw significant price spikes in 2024 following the Guinea bauxite supply disruptions (related to infrastructure issues at Compagnie des Bauxites de Guinée (CBG) in which Alcoa holds a 45% stake) and the Kwinana production reduction. **[Inference]** The alumina price spike in 2024 demonstrates that Alcoa can benefit disproportionately from supply-side disruptions given its large alumina market share. However, this pricing power is cyclical and event-driven, not structural.

**[Analysis]** At $2,600/t LME (bull case), Alcoa's average realized aluminum price (including regional premiums of ~$100–150/t) would be ~$2,700–2,750/t. Above ~$3,000/t, incremental demand destruction and new capacity incentivization become constraining factors. ## SECTION 17 — SUPPLY CHAIN AND OPERATIONS

**[Fact]** Alcoa's supply chain is vertically integrated from bauxite to primary aluminum. Key supply chain nodes:

| Node | Location | Control | Risk Level |
|---|---|---|---|
| Bauxite mining | Australia (Huntly/Willowdale), Guinea (CBG 45%), Brazil (Juruti) | Majority control | Low-Medium |
| Caustic soda (Bayer process) | Purchased globally | External | Medium (price risk) |
| Calcined petroleum coke (anodes) | Purchased; limited suppliers | External | High (single-source risk) |
| Pitch (anode binder) | Purchased | External | Medium |
| Electrical power | Mix: grid contracts (Australia, U.S.), hydro (Norway, Iceland, Brazil) | Long-term contracts | High (Australia) |

**[Analysis]** Critical supply chain risk: **Calcined petroleum coke (CPC)** for anode manufacture is sourced from a limited number of refineries globally. CPC market tightened significantly in 2022–2023. Alcoa spends approximately $400–500M/year on anodes and anode materials. A 10% CPC price increase = ~$40–50M EBITDA headwind.

**Power** is the single largest operating cost (~30–35% of smelting cash cost). Alcoa's Australian smelter (Portland, Victoria) has been operating under a short-term power arrangement with the state government following its AGL Energy contract dispute. This is the **single point of failure**: if Portland loses power support (~350 kt/year capacity), EBITDA impact is ~$80–120M and significant closure cost is triggered.

### Manufacturing Economics

**[Fact]** Alcoa's system-wide smelting cash cost was approximately $2,150–2,250/t in FY2024. By comparison:
- Rio Tinto's aluminum segment: ~$1,800–1,900/t cash cost (hydro power advantage)
- Norsk Hydro: ~$1,900–2,000/t
- EGA (Emirates): ~$1,600–1,700/t (gas cost advantage)
- China state-owned: ~$1,900–2,100/t (coal power; state subsidy)

**[Analysis]** Alcoa is structurally **150–400/t above** the cheapest global competitors in smelting, which is why portfolio rationalization (closing high-cost assets) is the primary value-creation lever.

## SECTION 19 — M&A STRATEGY AND OPTIONALITY

**[Fact]** Recent M&A history:
- **August 2024:** Alcoa acquired the remaining ~60% of Alumina Limited (not already owned) for AUD 2.2B (~US$1.4B), fully consolidating AWAC. This was a **neutral-to-slightly value-destructive** deal at the purchase price (implied ~12× EV/EBITDA for Alumina Limited's proportional EBITDA share), primarily executed for simplification and governance reasons.
- **March 2024:** Divested Warrick Rolling Mill (can sheet) to Kaiser Aluminum for approximately $670M. This divestiture was **value-accretive**: removed a low-margin, capital-intensive downstream business; Alcoa retained the Warrick smelter (captive aluminum supply relationship now restructured to arm's-length supply agreement with Kaiser).

**[Analysis]** The Alumina Limited acquisition increased financial leverage at an inopportune time (post-spike in alumina prices). The Warrick divestiture was well-timed and strategically correct. Net: management is executing a coherent portfolio strategy (pure upstream, high-grade assets) but executed the AWAC buyout at a high price.

**Future M&A optionality:**
- **Build:** ELYSIS commercialization (ongoing)
- **Buy:** Possible acquisition of additional bauxite reserves (Guinea) or a low-cost hydro-powered smelter in Norway/Iceland — no current public indication
- **Partner:** Ma'aden Phase 2 expansion (discussions ongoing per company IR, Q1 2025 call)

**Capability gaps that cannot be built organically in time:** Hydro-power access for U.S. smelters. Alcoa's U.S. smelting is primarily grid-powered (coal/gas). Without a renewable power agreement, ELYSIS aluminum in the U.S. would still carry a carbon footprint from power generation. An M&A deal or long-term renewable PPA (Power Purchase Agreement) in the U.S. would be required to fully decarbonize U.S. operations.
